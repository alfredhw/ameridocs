Regional collections spanning Arizona, New Mexico, and Texas. See also individual state pages.

Regional Digital Portals

  • The Portal to Texas History 2 million+ digitized items from or about Texas: photographs, maps, letters, documents, books, artifacts, and nearly 10 million digitized newspaper pages. Free online access to all materials.
  • New Mexico’s Digital Collections 100,000+ objects from UNM and 21 other New Mexico institutions: photographs, manuscripts, posters, oral histories, videos, maps, and books on New Mexico and the Southwest.
  • Arizona Memory Project Nearly 280,000 items from Arizona archives, museums, libraries, and cultural institutions: government documents, photographs, maps, newspapers, and multimedia resources.
  • Texas Archival Resources Online (TARO) Searchable finding aids from repositories across Texas including the Texas State Library, Daughters of the Republic of Texas Archives, and San Antonio Municipal Archives.

Indigenous Peoples of the Southwest

  • Northern Arizona University — Hopi Oral History Project Partnership between NAU Cline Library and the Hopi Cultural Preservation Office recording stories of continuity and change among the Hopi community during the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries.
  • University of Arizona — Indigenous History in the Borderlands Digitized primary sources on Apache, Tohono O’odham, and other Southwest Indigenous peoples: letters, field notes, correspondence, and oral histories.
  • University of New Mexico — American Indian Oral History Collection Transcripts of 1967–1972 recordings with Navajo individuals covering personal and family histories, culture, education, ceremonies, legends, religion, economy, and governance.

Spanish Colonial Period

  • Briscoe Center — Béxar Archives Online 250,000+ pages of Spanish and Mexican hand-written manuscripts (1717–1821) documenting the Spanish province of Texas and the Mexican state of Coahuila y Texas: mission records, military dispatches, and civil administration.
  • University of New Mexico — Spanish Archives of New Mexico Colonial Spanish manuscripts from the Archivo General de la Nación and other Mexican archives on the colonial history of New Mexico and New Spain (1598–1821): administrative, civil, military, and ecclesiastical records.
  • National Park Service — El Camino Real de Tierra Adentro Digital photographs and documentation along the 1,600-mile route of trade and cultural exchange connecting New Mexico to Mexico City—the lifeline of New Spain’s northern frontier.
  • DPLA — Spanish Missions Primary sources on the mission system across the Spanish borderlands, with materials relevant to Arizona and New Mexico missions including San Xavier del Bac and the Tumacácori missions.

The Mexican-American War & Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo

  • Library of Congress — Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo Manuscripts, government documents, maps, and newspaper articles on the 1848 treaty that ceded the Southwest to the United States and redefined the U.S.-Mexico border.
  • National Archives — Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo Full text of the treaty in Spanish and English, including original articles later modified by the Senate, with ratification history.
  • National Archives — Gadsden Purchase Treaty (1853) The treaty clarifying the southern boundary of New Mexico and Arizona, which completed the acquisition of the present-day continental United States.

The Borderlands

  • University of Arizona — The Documented Border Open-access digital archive from Special Collections focusing on untold and silenced stories of the U.S.-Mexico borderlands, with photographs, documents, and oral histories.
  • El Paso Public Library — Border Heritage Center Digital Collections 70+ archival collections documenting El Paso and the Southwest border: U.S. Army on the Border, Trost Architecture, and Russell Metcalfe photographs of border life.
  • Bracero History Archive Oral histories, documents, and photographs from the Bracero guest worker program (1942–1964), a collaboration of George Mason, the Smithsonian, Brown, and UT El Paso. (See also Labor & Industry.)
  • National Archives — Mexican Border Crossing Records Immigration records from Mexican border crossings (ca. 1903–1955): arrival records, naturalization documents, and border-crossing data.

The Nuclear Age

  • Atomic Heritage Foundation — Voices of the Manhattan Project 600 audio and video interviews with Manhattan Project workers and families, including J. Robert Oppenheimer, General Leslie Groves, and Glenn Seaborg—documenting Los Alamos, Trinity Site, and the birth of the nuclear age in the New Mexico desert.
  • Atomic Archive — Trinity Test Documentation, photographs, and background materials on the July 16, 1945 Trinity test at Alamogordo, New Mexico—the world’s first nuclear detonation.

Ranching & Cattle Culture

  • Texas State Library — Cattle Industry Records Documentation of the Texas cattle industry, trail drives, and the open-range era: brand records, trail boss accounts, and livestock association records.
  • Briscoe Center — Digital Collections Robert Runyon’s 8,000+ photographs of the South Texas border region and ranching culture, plus other Southwest visual collections.

Major Research Collections

  • Briscoe Center for American History — University of Texas at Austin The principal repository of Texas history primary sources: digital photographs, documents, maps, oral histories, and artifacts covering all aspects of Texas and Southwest history.
  • University of New Mexico — Center for Southwest Research 120,000+ images (mid-1850s to present), rare books, manuscripts, and ephemera specializing in New Mexico, the Southwest, Mexico, and Latin America.
  • SMU — DeGolyer Library 150,000 rare books, 2,500+ manuscript collections, 900,000 photographs, and 3,000 early maps, with notable strengths in Western Americana, Archives of Women of the Southwest (200+ collections), and railroad history.
  • University of Arizona — Special Collections Arizona and Southwest collections (est. 1958): the Arizona Index, historic Mexican-American press, land grants, ranching records, family papers, and the Arizona Queer Archives.
