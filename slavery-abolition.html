The Transatlantic Slave Trade

  • SlaveVoyages (Rice University) Comprehensive database of approximately 36,000 transatlantic slave trading voyages (1520–1866) with voyage logs, maps, 3D animation, and interactive analysis tools. The largest transatlantic slave trade database available.

Slavery in the Colonies & Early Republic

  • University of Virginia — Geography of Slavery in Virginia Runaway and captured slave advertisements from eighteenth- and nineteenth-century Virginia newspapers, with personal descriptions, geographic context, and documented resistance.
  • Cornell University — Freedom on the Move Crowdsourced database of approximately 100,000 fugitive slave advertisements from pre-1865 North American newspapers, searchable by appearance, clothing, family members, and destinations.
  • UNC Greensboro — Digital Library on American Slavery Database of tens of thousands of public records about 200,000+ people including enslaved, enslavers, and free people of color—one of the largest slave name databases online.
  • UNC Greensboro — Slave Deeds of North Carolina Digitized slave deeds and bills of sale documenting ownership transfers, sales records, and manumission documents from over 10,000 records.
  • Duke University — Rubenstein Library Slavery Documents 200+ items including bills of sale, rental agreements, and free papers, plus 600+ additional items documenting slavery-related transactions.

Slave Narratives & Testimony

  • Library of Congress — Born in Slavery: WPA Slave Narratives Over 2,300 interviews with formerly enslaved people (1936–1938) including transcripts and 500+ photographs—the largest collection of primary source materials from enslaved individuals.
  • UNC Chapel Hill — North American Slave Narratives (DocSouth) All known published narratives written by self-emancipated and formerly enslaved people as broadsides, pamphlets, and books through 1920.
  • Library of Congress — Voices Remembering Slavery Audio recordings of formerly enslaved people collected between 1932–1975 across nine states—testimony in their own voices.

The Abolition Movement

  • University of Rochester — Frederick Douglass Papers Comprehensive collection of Douglass correspondence, speeches, and biographical materials including over 100 letters from his 1846 UK visit and extensive correspondence with abolitionists.
  • Digital Commonwealth — The Liberator (William Lloyd Garrison) Complete digitized runs of the radical abolitionist weekly newspaper (1831–1865), searchable full-text.
  • Boston Public Library — Anti-Slavery Collection Approximately 40,000 pieces of antislavery correspondence, newspapers, broadsides, and organizational records from the American, Massachusetts, New England, and Female Anti-Slavery Societies.
  • University of Maryland — Freedmen and Southern Society Project ~50,000 transcribed documents from millions in the National Archives, covering the path from slavery to freedom with correspondence from enslaved, freed, and free Black people.

The Underground Railroad

  • National Underground Railroad Freedom Center Museum collections and the John Parker Library with multimedia materials about Underground Railroad routes and freedom seekers.
  • National Park Service — Harriet Tubman Underground Railroad Documented routes, safe houses, and biographical information on Harriet Tubman’s escape and approximately 13 rescue missions.
  • National Archives — Fugitive Slave Act Court Records Federal court case files, affidavits, and warrants relating to fugitive slaves (1851–1863) and enforcement of the Fugitive Slave Act.

The Civil War & Emancipation

  • National Archives — Emancipation Proclamation High-resolution images, full transcript, and detailed history of the original Emancipation Proclamation (January 1, 1863).
  • Smithsonian NMAAHC — Freedmen’s Bureau Search Portal Searchable database of nearly 1.8 million digitized Freedmen’s Bureau records transcribed by 25,000 volunteers through the Smithsonian Transcription Center.
  • National Archives — Freedmen’s Bureau Records Complete guide to Freedmen’s Bureau records with information on all field offices and organizational structure during Reconstruction (1865–1872).

Reconstruction

  • National Archives — 13th, 14th & 15th Amendments Full texts, historical background, and high-resolution images of the three Reconstruction Amendments abolishing slavery, guaranteeing citizenship, and protecting voting rights.
  • Equal Justice Initiative — Reconstruction in America Comprehensive documentation of racial violence during Reconstruction (1865–1876) including nearly 2,000 additional lynchings not previously documented.

Jim Crow, Segregation & Lynching

  • Equal Justice Initiative — Lynching in America Interactive Map Interactive mapping of 6,500+ documented racial terror lynchings (1865–1950) with victim profiles, geographic locations, and historical narratives.
  • National Archives — Plessy v. Ferguson (1896) Full Supreme Court ruling text legalizing “separate but equal” segregation, with historical context and educational materials.
  • University of Chicago — Ida B. Wells Papers Finding aid to the Wells papers (1884–1976): anti-lynching writings, diaries, correspondence, and activism documentation from the pioneering journalist and civil rights advocate.
  • Library of Congress — NAACP Anti-Lynching Campaign Photographs 98 photographic prints (1916–1964) documenting NAACP anti-lynching demonstrations, victims, families, and lobbying efforts.

The Civil Rights Movement

  • Stanford University — Martin Luther King, Jr. Research and Education Institute The King Papers Project with speeches, sermons, letters, manuscripts, and the searchable Online King Records Access (OKRA) database.
  • Duke University — SNCC Digital Gateway Free, open-access resource with primary documents, interviews, audio/visual files, and narratives documenting the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee’s grassroots activism from the perspective of SNCC veterans.
  • University of Georgia — Civil Rights Digital Library Over 55,000 digitized items from 200+ institutions with interactive mapping, biographical information on 3,000+ people, and news film archives.
  • American Archive of Public Broadcasting — Eyes on the Prize Interviews Over 300 raw footage interviews with civil rights participants conducted for the acclaimed 14-hour documentary series (1954–1985), with complete transcripts.
  • Wisconsin Historical Society — Mississippi Freedom Summer (1964) 100+ manuscript collections on the Freedom Summer Project: 750+ folders and over 100,000 pages of documents, with 25,000+ pages available online.

Major Research Collections

  • New York Public Library — Digital Schomburg Over 1,000 digitized collections with 17,000+ items on African and African diaspora history from the world’s leading research library on Black history.
  • Howard University — Moorland-Spingarn Research Center 700+ collections documenting the global Black experience; digital collections include the Black Press Archives (2,000+ newspaper titles with 100,000+ issues).
  • Smithsonian NMAAHC — Collections Searchable database of 45,000+ objects with digital collections, virtual exhibitions, and open-access images.
  • Tulane University — Amistad Research Center Nation’s oldest and largest independent archive on African American and ethnic minority history: 10 million documents, 250,000 photographs, 20,000+ books, and 1,000+ artworks.
  • Library of Congress — African American History Online Guide Comprehensive research guide to 400+ years of African American primary sources including the Douglass Papers, Mary Church Terrell Papers, Gladstone Photographs, and the Civil Rights History Project.
  • Library of Congress — Daniel A. P. Murray Collection 400+ rare digitized pamphlets on African American subjects published 1818–1907.
