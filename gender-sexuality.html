Major LGBTQ Archives & Digital Collections

  • ONE National Gay & Lesbian Archives at USC The largest LGBTQ archive in the world: 87,200+ digitized pages of community publications, personal papers, organizational records, photographs, and artwork from the 1920s to the present, housed at the USC Libraries.
  • Digital Transgender Archive 10,300+ materials from 70+ contributing institutions: periodicals, correspondence, photographs, organizational records, and ephemera documenting the history of gender-nonconforming and transgender communities.
  • GLBT Historical Society — Digital Collections Digitized materials from a San Francisco institution with one of the world’s largest LGBTQ collections: photographs, organizational records, periodicals, posters, and personal papers.
  • Lesbian Herstory Archives Brooklyn-based community archive preserving records of lesbian lives and activities: newsletters, photographs, organizational records, special collections on women of color, and the Daughters of Bilitis video project.
  • New York Public Library — LGBTQ Collections Materials across multiple NYPL divisions: the International Gay Information Center Archives, ACT UP records, personal papers of activists, and periodicals documenting New York’s central role in LGBTQ history.
  • Cornell University — Human Sexuality Collection Major research collection on the history of sexuality, sexual behavior, and sexology: rare publications, organizational records, personal papers, and visual materials from the nineteenth century to the present.
  • OutHistory Free, collaborative digital archive of LGBTQ history: timelines, primary source exhibits, community-contributed materials, and the Jonathan Ned Katz collection of documents on American LGBTQ life from the colonial era forward.

The Homophile Movement (1950s–1960s)

  • ONE Archives — ONE Magazine (1953–1967) Digitized issues of the first openly gay magazine continuously published in the United States, along with records of the Mattachine Society and ONE, Incorporated—foundational homophile organizations.
  • NYPL — Daughters of Bilitis Video Project Video oral histories with members of the first lesbian civil rights organization in the United States (founded 1955), documenting the pre-Stonewall era of lesbian organizing and The Ladder newsletter.

Stonewall & Gay Liberation

  • University at Buffalo — Gay Liberation Movement Primary sources on the post-Stonewall gay liberation movement: newsletters, pamphlets, photographs, and organizational records documenting early 1970s activism.

The AIDS Crisis & Activism

  • ACT UP Oral History Project 187 video interviews (5–8 hours each) with surviving members of the AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power, documenting the history of direct-action AIDS activism from 1987 through the 2000s.
  • USC — ONE Archives AIDS Collections Digitized materials from the AIDS crisis: newsletters, photographs, organizational records, and ephemera from community-based AIDS service organizations and activist groups in Los Angeles and nationwide.

Transgender History

  • Digital Transgender Archive The central digital hub for trans history: periodicals like Transvestia (1960–1986), correspondence, medical records, photographs, and organizational records from pioneering trans and gender-nonconforming communities.
  • GLBT Historical Society — Screaming Queens: The Riot at Compton’s Cafeteria Documentary materials on the 1966 Compton’s Cafeteria riot in San Francisco’s Tenderloin district, one of the first recorded transgender uprisings in the United States, predating Stonewall by three years.
  • Independent Voices: An Open Access Collection of an Alternative Press (JSTOR) 1,000+ alternative press periodicals (1951–2018) including gay liberation newspapers, lesbian feminist journals, and queer community publications documenting countercultural movements. Now hosted at JSTOR.
  • Cornell University — Hip Hop Collection 250,000+ items documenting hip hop history and culture, with materials relevant to the intersection of race, gender, sexuality, and popular culture in late twentieth-century America.

Marriage Equality & Legal History

  • Oyez — Obergefell v. Hodges (2015) Summary, oral arguments, and full text of the landmark decision establishing the constitutional right to same-sex marriage, with concurring and dissenting opinions.
  • Library of Congress — Civil Rights History Project Oral histories with LGBTQ rights activists among other civil rights leaders, documenting the legal and social struggles for equality from the mid-twentieth century onward.
  • Lambda Legal — Legal Documents Court filings, briefs, and legal documents from decades of LGBTQ civil rights litigation, including landmark cases on employment discrimination, military service, and relationship recognition.

Gender History & Feminism

  • Smith College — Sophia Smith Collection Major research archive on the history of women and gender: 900+ manuscript collections documenting feminism, reproductive rights, sexuality, and women’s activism from the nineteenth century to the present. (See also Women’s History.)
  • Schlesinger Library on the History of Women in America Manuscript collections, photographs, oral histories, and periodicals documenting women’s rights, gender, and feminist movements, with particular strengths in twentieth-century feminism and sexuality. (See also Women’s History.)
  • DPLA — Women and Work Primary sources on the intersection of gender and labor: photographs, documents, and artifacts on women’s changing economic roles and the gendered dimensions of American work.

Two-Spirit & Indigenous Gender Traditions

  • First Nations Development Institute Resources on contemporary Indigenous issues including Two-Spirit identity within Native communities, connecting historical gender traditions to present-day advocacy and cultural revitalization.
  • National Archives — Native American Records Federal records on Native nations with materials relevant to gender roles, marriage practices, and social organization in tribal communities as documented by Bureau of Indian Affairs agents and ethnographers. (See also Native Nations.)
