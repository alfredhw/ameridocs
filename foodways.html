Historic Cookbooks & Recipe Collections

  • Michigan State University — Feeding America: The Historic American Cookbook Project 76 of the most influential and important American cookbooks (1798–1922) digitized in full, with searchable text—tracing the evolution of American cuisine from the early Republic through the Progressive Era.
  • New York Public Library — Rare Book Division: Early American Cookbooks 1,450 titles published between 1800 and 1920 from the NYU/NYPL digitization project: regional, ethnic, institutional, and domestic cookbooks documenting American food culture.
  • Internet Archive — Community Cookbooks 12,700+ digitized cookbooks spanning American history: community fundraiser cookbooks, household manuals, etiquette guides, and commercial recipe pamphlets revealing local foodways across the country.
  • Utah State University — Fife Folklore Archive: Cookery Collection Historic cookbooks and food-related publications from the Fife Folklore Archive, with particular strength in Western and Mormon community cookbooks and homemaking guides.
  • University of Michigan — Janice Bluestein Longone Culinary Archive 30,000+ items constituting one of the nation’s finest culinary collections: cookbooks, menus, trade cards, food packaging, advertisements, and manuscripts documenting American food from the eighteenth century to the present.
  • Internet Archive — Johnson & Wales Culinary Arts Museum 33,000 books, menus, and ephemera from one of America’s largest culinary collections, now accessible through the Internet Archive.

Indigenous Foodways

  • USDA National Agricultural Library — The Three Sisters Exhibit and primary sources on the foundational Native American agricultural practice of interplanting corn, beans, and squash—a cultivation system that sustained Indigenous peoples for millennia and influenced colonial agriculture.
  • Library of Congress — American Folklife Center Ethnographic collections including field recordings, photographs, and manuscripts documenting food traditions among Native American, African American, and immigrant communities.

Agricultural History & Food Production

  • USDA — National Agricultural Library Digital Collections Digitized USDA publications, research bulletins, and extension materials from the 1860s to the present: crop reports, nutritional studies, food safety regulations, and agricultural science.
  • USDA National Agricultural Library — George Washington Carver Papers, photographs, and publications of George Washington Carver documenting his research on peanuts, sweet potatoes, and Southern agriculture at Tuskegee Institute—a story of food innovation, race, and agricultural science.
  • Cornell University — USDA Census of Agriculture Historical Census of Agriculture reports (1840–2017) documenting the transformation of American farming: crop production, livestock, farm size, land use, and the industrialization of the food system. (See also Economic & Commercial History.)
  • DPLA — The Cotton Gin and the Expansion of Slavery Primary sources on how agricultural technology reshaped American foodways and labor: the cotton gin’s impact on plantation agriculture, the slave economy, and Southern food culture. (See also Slavery & Abolition.)

Southern & Regional Foodways

  • Southern Foodways Alliance — Oral Histories 700+ oral history interviews documenting Southern food culture: barbecue traditions, Lowcountry rice culture, Cajun and Creole cooking, Appalachian foodways, and the African American culinary heritage at the heart of Southern cuisine.
  • Smithsonian Center for Folklife and Cultural Heritage — Archives Recordings, photographs, and festival documentation from the Smithsonian Folklife Festival and fieldwork projects, with extensive materials on regional American food traditions, craft brewing, and culinary folk arts.
  • DPLA — Colonial Foodways Primary sources on food in colonial America: descriptions of Native American agriculture, early European accounts of New World foods, plantation provisioning, and the Columbian Exchange.

Immigrant Foodways

  • Library of Congress — Immigrant Arrivals at Ellis Island Passenger lists and photographs with materials relevant to the foodways immigrants brought to America: Italian, Jewish, Chinese, and other culinary traditions that transformed the American diet. (See also Immigration.)
  • University of Minnesota — Immigration History Research Center Archives Ethnic community records, newspapers, and organizational papers documenting how immigrant groups maintained and adapted food traditions in the Midwest and beyond. (See also Immigration.)

Food Safety & Regulation

  • FDA — History of the FDA Historical materials on food safety regulation from the 1906 Pure Food and Drug Act to the present: photographs, documents, and narratives on the evolution of food regulation in America.
  • Newberry Library — The Jungle and the Community Documents on Chicago’s meatpacking industry (ca. 1901–1911) providing context for Upton Sinclair’s exposé and the reform movement that led to federal food inspection. (See also Labor & Industry.)

Reference & Research Tools

  • Library of Congress — Food and Foodways Web Archive Archived websites on American food culture, agriculture, nutrition policy, food justice, and culinary heritage—preserving born-digital food history resources.
  • The Food Timeline Reference site tracing the historical origins of foods and food customs, with sourced entries on when specific dishes, ingredients, and food traditions appeared in American culture.
