Home › New England

Regional collections spanning Connecticut, Maine, Massachusetts, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, and Vermont. See also individual state pages.

Regional Digital Portals

  • Digital Commonwealth Collaborative digital library connecting 220+ Massachusetts libraries, museums, archives, and historical societies with photographs, manuscripts, audio recordings, and other digitized materials.
  • Maine Memory Network 45,000+ digitized primary sources from 270+ Maine organizations: letters, journals, government records, photographs, maps, and artifacts, with many fully transcribed.
  • Connecticut Digital Archive (CTDA) Preserved digital materials from Connecticut cultural institutions including the Barnum Museum, Hartford Medical Society, Litchfield Historical Society, and Wadsworth Atheneum.
  • Historic New England — Collections Access Searchable database of 125,000+ objects and 1.5 million archival materials spanning the seventeenth century to the present: photographs, wallpaper, architectural drawings, clothing, and furnishings from across all six states.

Colonial New England

  • Congregational Library — New England’s Hidden Histories 130,000+ digital images and 26,000+ transcribed pages of manuscript church records (1620–1850) from 160+ historic New England churches: conversion narratives, disciplinary cases, baptisms, marriages, deaths, and account books.
  • Massachusetts Historical Society — Digital Collections 6,000+ digitized items from one of America’s oldest historical societies (est. 1791), including Adams family papers, colonial governance documents, and Revolutionary-era manuscripts.
  • American Antiquarian Society Worcester-based institution (est. 1812) with 4+ million original pre-1900 American sources: colonial diaries, personal papers, sheet music (60,000+ pieces), photographs, children’s literature (back to the 1650s), and digital exhibitions.
  • Early American Sources Portal aggregating digitized primary sources across multiple states, with dedicated sections for Massachusetts, Connecticut, New Hampshire, and New York archives spanning the colonial period through the Civil War.

Maritime & Whaling

  • WhalingHistory.org Multi-institutional collaboration among the Nantucket Atheneum, Mystic Seaport Museum, New Bedford Whaling Museum, and Nantucket Historical Association: American Offshore Whaling Voyages, crew databases, and digitized logbooks from across the New England whaling industry.
  • New Bedford Whaling Museum — Digital Collections 100,000+ object records from the Old Dartmouth Historical Society’s 1+ million-item collection, including rare books, manuscripts, oral histories, and marine mammal sound recordings.
  • Nantucket Atheneum — Whale Fishery Databases 2,500 voyage records in 900+ vessels and nearly 16,000 crew entries documenting the British and American whale fisheries (1775–1859).
  • Mystic Seaport Museum — Collections & Research 2+ million maritime artifacts: 1 million manuscript pieces (logbooks, journals, business records), 1,000 ships registers, 600+ oral histories, and nearly 1 million Rosenfeld maritime photographs (1881–1992).
  • Peabody Essex Museum — Phillips Library Digital Collections Digitized logbooks, account books, maps, architectural drawings, and photographs from Salem’s maritime heritage, including the Frank Cousins Collection and Essex County trade materials.

Industrialization & Textile Mills

  • UMass Lowell — Tsongas Industrial History Center Digital access to mill-related collections, virtual tours, and primary sources documenting the lives of Lowell’s textile workers (1820–1880), including the famous “mill girls” system that pioneered American industrial labor.
  • Old Slater Mill Association — Industrial Archives Archives from America’s first successful cotton mill (Pawtucket, Rhode Island, 1793), documenting the spread of water-powered textile manufacturing across New England—78+ new mills by 1812.
  • DPLA — The Lowell System and the Market Revolution Curated primary sources on the transformation from agrarian to industrial labor in early nineteenth-century New England.

Abolitionism & Reform

  • UMass Amherst — Antislavery Collection 100+ printed pamphlets and books (1725–1911) on slavery and antislavery in New England, including speeches and proceedings from the American Anti-Slavery Society and American Colonization Society.
  • Project Gutenberg — Frothingham, Transcendentalism in New England: A History Full text of the 1876 history of the transcendentalist movement and its intersection with abolitionism, women’s rights, and social reform across the region.

Immigration & Ethnic Communities

  • Rhode Island College — Cape Verdean Collections Multi-institutional collection documenting Cape Verdean immigration to New England: postcards, photographs, organizational papers (1945–2011), and materials on language, music, and community development.
  • American Ancestors (New England Historic Genealogical Society) 3,000+ searchable databases covering 113+ million people: Massachusetts Vital Records, the New England Historical and Genealogical Register, Great Migration project (1620–1633), and cemetery transcriptions spanning all six states.

University & Scholarly Collections

  • Brown University — Digital Repository Digital assets including the John Carter Brown Library’s outstanding collection of “New World” primary sources (late fifteenth to mid-nineteenth century), with particular strengths in colonial and early American materials.
  • Dartmouth College — Digital Collections Nearly 300,000 digitized items including New Hampshire maps (ca. 1700–2006), the Samson Occom Collection (1740s–1790s, Dartmouth’s founder), and Rauner Special Collections manuscripts.
  • University of Vermont — Center for Digital Initiatives Unique Vermont and New England collections: photographs, Civil War letters, and regional cultural heritage materials with ongoing digitization partnerships.
  • Portsmouth Athenaeum — Online Collections 40,000+ volumes and 12,000+ photographs (4,000 digitized) from New Hampshire’s independent membership library, including papers of Governor John Langdon, Declaration signer William Whipple, and Isles of Shoals materials.
