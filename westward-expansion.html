Early Exploration & the Corps of Discovery

  • University of Nebraska–Lincoln — Journals of the Lewis and Clark Expedition The definitive Gary E. Moulton edition of the complete Lewis and Clark journals (1804–1806) with maps, annotations, and full-text search—the primary documentary record of the Corps of Discovery.
  • Library of Congress — Thomas Jefferson Papers Over 27,000 documents including Jefferson’s instructions to Lewis, correspondence about the Louisiana Purchase, and planning documents for western exploration.
  • National Archives — Louisiana Purchase Treaty (1803) Full text and facsimile of the treaty that doubled the size of the United States and opened the trans-Mississippi West.
  • Library of Congress — John C. Frémont Papers Papers of “The Pathfinder” whose published reports and maps of the West (1842–1854) guided thousands of emigrants along the overland trails.

Overland Trails & Emigrant Journeys

  • Brigham Young University — Overland Trails Database Comprehensive collection of emigrant trail diaries, journals, and letters from travelers on the Oregon, California, and Mormon Trails, with maps and biographical information.
  • Idaho State University — Overland Trail Encyclopedic Chronology Detailed year-by-year chronology of overland emigration with primary source excerpts and historical context.
  • National Park Service — Oregon National Historic Trail Historical documentation, maps, and primary sources for the 2,170-mile route from Independence, Missouri, to Oregon’s Willamette Valley.
  • National Park Service — California National Historic Trail Primary sources and historical documentation for the emigrant routes to California, including Gold Rush–era trail accounts.
  • National Park Service — Mormon Pioneer National Historic Trail Documentation of the 1,300-mile route traveled by tens of thousands of Latter-day Saints from Illinois to Utah (1846–1869).

Land Policy & Homesteading

  • Bureau of Land Management — General Land Office Records Over 12 million federal land title records from 1788 to the present, including homestead patents, cash entries, military bounty land warrants, and survey plats—the definitive record of public land disposition.
  • National Archives — Homestead Act (1862) Full text and historical context of the landmark legislation that granted 160-acre parcels of public land to settlers, ultimately distributing 270 million acres.
  • National Archives — Land Records Research Guide Comprehensive guide to federal land records including bounty-land warrants, donation land claims, homestead applications, and private land claims.
  • DPLA — Homesteading in the United States Curated primary source set with photographs, documents, maps, and accounts of the homesteading experience across the Great Plains and West.
  • Library of Congress — A Century of Lawmaking Congressional records including debates on the Preemption Act (1841), Homestead Act (1862), and other land legislation that shaped western settlement.

The California Gold Rush (1848–1855)

  • Library of Congress — California as I Saw It: First-Person Narratives (1849–1900) Over 190 digitized works by travelers to California including miners, missionaries, and settlers, with eyewitness accounts of Gold Rush life.
  • Calisphere — California Gold Rush Collections Aggregated Gold Rush materials from California libraries, archives, and museums including photographs, diaries, maps, and business records.
  • UC Berkeley — Bancroft Library Californiana Collections One of the world’s largest collections of California and western history materials, including Gold Rush diaries, mining company records, and early California government documents.
  • California Historical Society — Collections Photographs, manuscripts, and ephemera documenting Gold Rush–era California, including mining camp life, immigration, and urban development.

The Transcontinental Railroad

  • DPLA — Transcontinental Railroad Curated primary sources including photographs, maps, broadsides, and government documents on the construction and impact of the first transcontinental railroad (completed 1869).
  • Stanford University — Chinese Railroad Workers in North America Project Research project recovering the experiences of 15,000–20,000 Chinese workers who built the Central Pacific Railroad, with primary sources, archaeological findings, and descendant stories.
  • Library of Congress — “Across the Continent” Railroad Prints Visual primary sources including Currier & Ives prints and photographs depicting the construction, celebration, and cultural impact of the transcontinental railroad.
  • National Archives — Pacific Railway Act (1862) Full text and context of the legislation authorizing construction of the transcontinental railroad and granting enormous land subsidies to railroad companies.

Great Surveys & Western Mapping

  • USGS — History of the U.S. Geological Survey Documentation of the four Great Surveys of the West (King, Wheeler, Hayden, Powell) that mapped the interior (1867–1879) and led to the creation of the USGS.
  • Library of Congress — William Henry Jackson Photographs Photographs from the Hayden Survey (1870s) of Yellowstone and the Rocky Mountain West that helped convince Congress to establish Yellowstone National Park.
  • Library of Congress — Maps of North America Historical map collections documenting the evolving understanding of western geography from the colonial era through the surveys of the nineteenth century.
  • Library of Congress — John Wesley Powell’s Report on the Lands of the Arid Region (1878) Powell’s influential report proposing that western settlement follow watershed boundaries, a prescient analysis of arid-lands water management.

Indian Wars & Displacement

  • DPLA — The Wounded Knee Massacre (1890) Documents, photographs, government records, and newspaper accounts of the 1890 massacre of Lakota people at Wounded Knee Creek.
  • National Park Service — Sand Creek Massacre (1864) Historical documentation of the massacre of Cheyenne and Arapaho people by Colorado militia, with primary sources and educational materials.
  • National Archives — Indian Wars Military Records Guide to military service records, muster rolls, pension files, and correspondence from conflicts between the U.S. Army and Native peoples (1784–1898).
  • DPLA — Cherokee Removal and the Trail of Tears Primary sources on Cherokee removal, including letters, correspondence from tribal leaders, and documentation of the forced march west. (See also Native Nations.)

Territorial Governance

  • Library of Congress — Territorial Papers of the United States Official records of territorial governance from the Northwest Ordinance (1787) through the admission of western territories as states.
  • National Archives — Records of the Office of the Secretary of the Interior (RG 48) Territorial papers, correspondence, and administrative records for western territories under Interior Department oversight.
  • DPLA — Oklahoma Land Rush (1889–1895) Primary sources on the land runs that opened former Indian Territory to non-Native settlement, including photographs, maps, and personal accounts.

Ranching, Cowboys & the Open Range

  • Library of Congress — National Cowboy & Western Heritage Museum Collection Photographs, manuscripts, and ephemera documenting ranching, rodeo, and Western culture from the open-range era to the twentieth century.
  • Texas State Library — Cattle Industry Records Documentation of the Texas cattle industry, trail drives, and the open-range era including brand records, trail boss accounts, and livestock association records.
  • Kansas Historical Society — Cattle Trails of Kansas Primary sources on the great cattle trails through Kansas (Chisholm, Western, Shawnee) with maps, photographs, and contemporary accounts of the droving era.

The Frontier in American Thought

  • American Historical Association — Turner’s “Significance of the Frontier” (1893) Full text of Frederick Jackson Turner’s influential address at the 1893 World’s Columbian Exposition, which argued that the frontier shaped American democracy and character.
  • U.S. Census Bureau — 1890 Census Facts The 1890 Census declared the frontier “closed,” the announcement that prompted Turner’s thesis and marked a symbolic end of westward expansion.

Major Research Collections

  • Newberry Library — Everett D. Graff Collection of Western Americana Over 10,000 printed items and manuscripts documenting the American West from the sixteenth century through 1900, including overland narratives, fur trade accounts, and settlement records.
  • Library of Congress — Western Expansion Collections Curated gateway to LOC’s western expansion holdings including maps, photographs, manuscripts, and printed materials documenting the movement west.
  • Yale University — Beinecke Western Americana Collection Major research collection spanning the colonial era to the twentieth century, with strengths in exploration, Native American materials, mining, ranching, and the overland trails.
  • Autry Museum of the American West — Research Collections Over 600,000 objects and 500,000 photographs documenting the diverse peoples of the American West, with digitized collections available online.
