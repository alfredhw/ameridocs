Scope. This page covers African American history broadly—cultural movements, institutions, intellectual life, and contemporary issues. For slavery, abolition, the Underground Railroad, and the civil rights movement, see Slavery, Abolition & the African American Experience. For the Great Migration, see Immigration & Migration. The Harlem Renaissance & Black Cultural Movements

  • New York Public Library — Digital Schomburg Over 1,000 digitized collections with 17,000+ items from the world’s leading research library on Black history, with extensive holdings on the Harlem Renaissance: manuscripts, photographs, periodicals, and artwork from the 1920s–1930s.
  • Digital Public Library of America — The Harlem Renaissance Curated primary source set with photographs, artworks, publications, and documents exploring the literary, artistic, and intellectual explosion of Black culture in 1920s Harlem.
  • Yale Beinecke Library — James Weldon Johnson Memorial Collection One of the premier archives of African American arts and letters: manuscripts, correspondence, photographs, and recordings from Langston Hughes, Zora Neale Hurston, Claude McKay, Countee Cullen, and other Harlem Renaissance figures.
  • Smithsonian Archives of American Art — Harmon Foundation Records Records of the organization (1922–1967) that sponsored African American art exhibitions and awards, including correspondence, exhibition catalogs, photographs, and documentation of artists such as Aaron Douglas, Augusta Savage, and Jacob Lawrence.
  • University of Florida — Zora Neale Hurston Digital Collection Digitized manuscripts, photographs, correspondence, and research materials from the novelist, folklorist, and anthropologist whose work documented African American vernacular culture in the rural South and Caribbean.

Historically Black Colleges & Universities (HBCUs)

  • Tuskegee University — Booker T. Washington Digital Collection Papers, correspondence, and materials documenting Washington’s founding and leadership of Tuskegee Institute (1881–1915). (See also Alabama.)
  • Howard University — Moorland-Spingarn Research Center One of the world’s largest repositories documenting the global Black experience: 175,000+ books and periodicals, the Black Press Archives (2,000+ newspaper titles), and 700+ manuscript collections. (See also Slavery & Abolition.)
  • Atlanta University Center — Robert W. Woodruff Library Digital Collections Digitized materials from Morehouse College, Spelman College, Clark Atlanta University, and the Interdenominational Theological Center: yearbooks, newspapers, photographs, institutional records, and papers documenting Black higher education since the 1860s.
  • Library of Congress — Booker T. Washington Papers Correspondence, memoranda, book drafts, articles, speeches, and reports (1853–1946) from the most prominent African American educator and political figure of the late nineteenth century.
  • Fisk University — Special Collections Archival holdings from one of the nation’s oldest HBCUs (founded 1866), including W.E.B. Du Bois papers, Jubilee Singers materials, and the Fisk University Galleries’ collection of African American art.

The Black Press & Media

  • Library of Congress — Chronicling America (African American Newspapers) Over 20 million searchable newspaper pages include dozens of African American titles: The Colored American, The Appeal, The New York Age, and others. Filterable by ethnicity and state.
  • Internet Archive — African American Periodicals Collection Digitized periodicals including The Crisis (NAACP, founded 1910 by W.E.B. Du Bois), Opportunity: Journal of Negro Life (National Urban League, 1923–1949), and other magazines documenting Black intellectual and cultural life.
  • Umbra Search African American History Federated search across hundreds of thousands of digitized materials from 1,000+ libraries and archives, including Black newspapers, church records, and organizational publications.
  • Library of Congress — The African American Odyssey Major exhibition drawing on the LOC’s extensive African American collections: rare books, manuscripts, prints, photographs, and sound recordings spanning the full arc of the Black American experience.

African American Music: Jazz, Blues, Gospel & Spirituals

  • Smithsonian Jazz (National Museum of American History) The museum’s major jazz collections including the Duke Ellington Collection (200,000+ pages of scores, parts, and manuscripts), the Ella Fitzgerald Collection, and holdings documenting the history of jazz from New Orleans to the present.
  • Tulane University — Hogan Jazz Archive Over 2,000 oral history interviews, 500,000 photographs, 30,000 sound recordings, and extensive sheet music documenting jazz from its New Orleans origins through the present.
  • Smithsonian Folkways Recordings Over 4,000 albums (60,000+ tracks) available for streaming, including landmark recordings of African American blues, gospel, spirituals, jazz, and folk music. Includes the historic Folkways Records catalog (founded 1948) with Lead Belly recordings, work songs, and sacred music.
  • University of Mississippi — Blues Archive Over 60,000 sound recordings, photographs, books, and personal papers: one of the largest blues collections in the world, including the B.B. King Collection and materials documenting the Mississippi Delta blues tradition.
  • Library of Congress — African American Spirituals Digitized field recordings, sheet music, and scholarly essays documenting the slave spiritual tradition—the foundational form of African American sacred music. (See also American Religious History.)

The Black Power Movement

  • Internet Archive — COINTELPRO Documents (FBI via FOIA) Declassified FBI Counter Intelligence Program documents (1956–1971) detailing the government’s surveillance and disruption campaigns against the Black Panther Party, SCLC, Nation of Islam, and other Black organizations.
  • NYPL Schomburg Center — Digital Collections Extensive materials on the Black Power movement: pamphlets, posters, photographs, and organizational records from the Black Panther Party, the Republic of New Afrika, and other Black nationalist organizations of the 1960s–1970s.
  • Stanford King Institute — Black Power Era Primary source materials on the Black Power movement, including documents from SNCC’s evolution toward Black Power, Stokely Carmichael materials, and the broader context of Black nationalist thought and its relationship to the civil rights movement.
  • Duke University — Behind the Veil: Documenting African American Life in the Jim Crow South Over 1,200 oral history interviews conducted across 10 southern states, documenting Black community life, religious institutions, and everyday resistance under segregation from the 1890s through the 1950s. Audio and transcripts available.

African American Art & Literature

  • Smithsonian American Art Museum — African American Art Searchable online collection with thousands of works by African American artists: paintings, sculpture, photography, and mixed media from the nineteenth century to the present, including works by Jacob Lawrence, Alma Thomas, Sam Gilliam, and Romare Bearden.
  • Studio Museum in Harlem Over 2,400 works of art by artists of African descent in a searchable online database. Founded 1968, the museum is the premier institution dedicated to visual art by people of African descent, with a collection spanning the nineteenth century to the present.
  • Tulane University — Amistad Research Center Nation’s oldest and largest independent archive on African American and ethnic minority history: 10 million documents, 250,000 photographs, 20,000+ books, and 1,000+ artworks. (See also Slavery & Abolition.)
  • DPLA — African American Authors and Their Literary Traditions Curated primary source set documenting the African American literary tradition with writings, photographs, and contextual materials from partner institutions across the country.

Black Women’s History

  • Library of Congress — Rosa Parks Papers Approximately 7,500 items (1866–2006): correspondence, writings, notes, and photographs documenting Parks’s life and activism beyond the Montgomery Bus Boycott, including her NAACP work, years in Detroit, and continued activism through the 1990s.
  • Library of Congress — Mary Church Terrell Papers Papers of the suffragist, educator, and civil rights activist who co-founded the NAACP and the National Association of Colored Women. (See also Women’s History.)
  • Schlesinger Library — Black Women Oral History Project 72 oral history interviews (1976–1981) with Black women who made significant contributions to American society in the early-to-mid twentieth century, including educators, activists, artists, and professionals.
  • National Archives — Mary McLeod Bethune Records of Bethune’s role as Director of the Division of Negro Affairs of the National Youth Administration (1936–1944), her founding of Bethune-Cookman College, and her leadership of the “Black Cabinet” advising FDR.

African American Science, Medicine & Innovation

  • National Archives at Atlanta — Tuskegee Syphilis Study Records Federal records documenting the U.S. Public Health Service Syphilis Study at Tuskegee (1932–1972): correspondence, medical records, photographs, and administrative documents from one of the most infamous cases of medical racism in American history.
  • Tuskegee University — George Washington Carver Digital Collection Complete Carver Bulletins and research documents from the agricultural scientist’s transformative work at Tuskegee. (See also Alabama and American Foodways.)
  • National Library of Medicine — Changing the Face of Medicine Exhibition and digital collection documenting pioneering physicians, including profiles and primary sources related to African American women and men in medicine and the broader context of racial barriers in medical education.
  • Smithsonian Lemelson Center for the Study of Invention and Innovation Resources documenting African American invention and innovation, including materials on patent records, inventor biographies, and the history of Black entrepreneurship and technical achievement.

Contemporary Issues & Post–Civil Rights Era

  • Equal Justice Initiative — Reports and Resources Free reports on mass incarceration, racial injustice, and the death penalty, including Slavery to Mass Incarceration. EJI documents the continuum from slavery through convict leasing and Jim Crow to the modern criminal justice system.
  • The Sentencing Project — Research Data, reports, and publications on racial disparities in the criminal justice system, mass incarceration, and sentencing policy, with state-level data, policy briefs, and interactive tools.
  • Smithsonian NMAAHC — Talking About Race Educational resources and primary source materials addressing contemporary racial issues with historical context for discussions of systemic racism, reparations, and racial equity.
  • Smithsonian NMAAHC — Collections Searchable database of 45,000+ objects covering all periods of African American history, with digital collections, virtual exhibitions, and open-access images.

Major Research Collections

  • Library of Congress — African American History Online Guide Comprehensive research guide to 400+ years of African American primary sources: the Douglass Papers, Mary Church Terrell Papers, Gladstone Photographs, the Civil Rights History Project, and more.
  • Library of Congress — Daniel A. P. Murray Collection 400+ rare digitized pamphlets on African American subjects published 1818–1907, documenting Black intellectual, political, and cultural life before the Harlem Renaissance.
  • Duke University — John Hope Franklin Research Center Major research center for African and African American history and culture within the Rubenstein Library, with extensive manuscript collections, photographs, and organizational records.
  • College of Charleston — Avery Research Center 200+ manuscript collections on African American history in the South Carolina Lowcountry: family papers predating the Civil War, freedom records, and antebellum free people of color documentation. (See also South Carolina.)
